### Grammar: Conditional Tenses

| Type | Use | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Zero** | General truths, scientific facts | If + present, present | If you heat water, it **boils** |
| **First** | Real/possible future | If + present, will + base | If it rains, I **will stay** home |
| **Second** | Unreal/hypothetical present | If + past simple, would + base | If I **won** the lottery, I **would travel** |
| **Third** | Unreal past (regret) | If + past perfect, would have + past participle | If I **had studied**, I **would have passed** |
| **Mixed** | Mix of second and third | Various combinations | If I **were** taller (present unreal), I **would have played** basketball (past result) |

**Key distinction**: second conditional = unlikely/imaginary now. Third = impossible past.

### Discussion: "But is it art?"

## 3. OSCAR WILDE (1854-1900)

### Quick Facts

- **Born**: Dublin, Ireland, 1854
- **Education**: Trinity College Dublin, then Oxford
- **Movement**: Leader of **Aestheticism** ("art for art's sake")
- **Known for**: brilliant wit, flamboyant style, plays, one novel
- **Famous works**: *The Picture of Dorian Gray* (1890), *The Importance of Being Earnest* (1895)
- **Scandal**: Imprisoned for "gross indecency" (homosexuality was illegal at the time)
- **Death**: 1900 in Paris, poor and forgotten

### Aestheticism — "Art for Art's Sake"

Wilde believed that art should be **beautiful and useless**. Art has no moral or didactic purpose — it exists only to be admired. It should not teach lessons or be judged by its morality. This was a direct challenge to the Victorian belief that literature should be morally improving.

**Key idea**: "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all."

### The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890)

**Plot**:
- Dorian Gray is a young, handsome man who has his portrait painted by artist Basil Hallward
- Influenced by Lord Henry Wotton (a cynical hedonist), Dorian wishes that the portrait would age instead of him
- The wish comes true: Dorian stays young while the portrait grows old and ugly, reflecting his sins
- Dorian lives a life of pleasure, corruption, and moral decay
- When he finally stabs the portrait, he dies — and the portrait returns to its original beauty, while Dorian's body becomes hideous

**Themes**:
- **Art vs. Life**: the relationship between beauty, morality, and reality
- **The double life**: public respectability vs. private corruption (Victorian hypocrisy)
- **Hedonism**: the pursuit of pleasure without moral limits
- **Aestheticism**: the book itself became evidence against Wilde at his trial — people argued it was an "immoral" book

**Famous Quotes**:
- "I can resist anything except temptation."
- "Experience is merely the name men give to their mistakes."
- "To define is to limit."

## ORAL EXAM TIPS — DAY 1

When talking about Oscar Wilde at the exam:
1. Start with: "Oscar Wilde was an Irish writer and the leading figure of the Aesthetic movement in England."
2. Explain Aestheticism clearly: "art for art's sake — beauty is the only purpose of art, not morality"
3. Summarize Dorian Gray in 3-4 sentences
4. Link to the technology/art question: "Today, AI-generated art raises the same question Wilde asked 130 years ago: what IS art?"
