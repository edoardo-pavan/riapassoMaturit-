> **Template**: "Today I'm going to talk about [topic]. Let me start with the positive impact. [2-3 positive points]. However, there are also drawbacks. [2-3 negative points]. In conclusion, I believe that..."

### A. Technology in Education

**Positive impact**:
- **Access to resources**: anyone with internet can access lectures, articles, tutorials (YouTube, Coursera, Khan Academy)
- **Distance learning**: education possible from anywhere (became essential during COVID-19)
- **Online collaboration**: students can work together remotely (Google Docs, Microsoft Teams)
- **Personalized learning**: AI adapts content to each student's level

**Negative impact**:
- **Distraction**: social media, notifications, games compete for attention
- **Digital divide**: not everyone has a computer or fast internet → inequality in education
- **Plagiarism**: easy to copy-paste, AI-generated essays
- **Screen fatigue**: too much time on devices affects health and concentration

**Vocabulary to use**: e-learning platform, digital literacy, blended learning, flipped classroom, Massive Open Online Course (MOOC).

---

### B. Technology in Music

**Positive impact**:
- **Easy access**: millions of songs on Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube (democratization of music)
- **New creative tools**: digital instruments, DAWs (Digital Audio Workstations) like Ableton, Logic Pro
- **AI-generated music**: new forms of creativity, tools that help composition
- **Independent artists**: can publish without a record label (SoundCloud, Bandcamp)

**Negative impact**:
- **Loss of income for artists**: streaming pays fractions of a cent per play
- **Piracy**: illegal downloads and file sharing
- **Copyright issues**: AI trained on artists' work without permission
- **Homogenization**: algorithms push similar-sounding music (the "Spotify sound")

**Vocabulary**: streaming platform, royalties, copyright infringement, DAW, algorithm, playlisting.

---

### C. Technology in Health

**Positive impact**:
- **Telemedicine**: doctors can consult patients remotely (video calls, apps)
- **Wearable devices**: smartwatches track heart rate, sleep, activity (Apple Watch, Fitbit)
- **Better diagnosis**: AI analyzes medical images (X-rays, MRIs) faster and sometimes more accurately than humans
- **Medical databases**: researchers can analyze massive datasets to find patterns and cures
- **Health apps**: help patients manage chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension)

**Negative impact**:
- **Privacy concerns**: sensitive health data could be leaked or misused
- **Over-reliance on devices**: people self-diagnose instead of seeing a doctor
- **False alarms**: wearables can cause unnecessary anxiety
- **Inequality of access**: expensive devices and apps not available to everyone
- **Data security**: medical records are a prime target for hackers

**Vocabulary**: telemedicine, wearable, biometric data, electronic health record (EHR), mHealth (mobile health), HIPAA (US health data law) / GDPR (EU).

---

### D. Technology in Cybersecurity

**Positive impact**:
- **Stronger protection**: encryption, firewalls, biometric authentication (face, fingerprint)
- **Secure transactions**: online banking, e-commerce with SSL/TLS
- **Real-time threat detection**: AI-powered systems monitor for suspicious activity 24/7
- **Privacy tools**: VPNs, encrypted messaging (Signal, WhatsApp)
- **Global cooperation**: international agencies fight cybercrime together

**Negative impact**:
- **New forms of cybercrime**: ransomware, phishing, identity theft are constantly evolving
- **Arms race**: security measures improve, but so do attacks (AI-powered hacking)
- **Constant need for updates**: "patch Tuesday" — software must be continuously updated
- **False sense of security**: people think they're safe when they're not
- **Privacy vs. security debate**: governments want backdoors, but that weakens security for everyone

**Vocabulary**: encryption, firewall, biometrics, two-factor authentication (2FA), VPN, phishing, malware, ransomware, zero-day exploit, penetration testing.

## 2. ED. CIVICA ESSAY — INTERNATIONAL INSTITUTIONS

### The Six Institutions — Quick Reference

| Institution | Full Name | Founded | HQ | Members | Key Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **EU** | European Union | 1993 (Maastricht Treaty) | Brussels (BE) | 27 countries | Political and economic union of European states. Single market, common currency (€), free movement. |
| **NATO** | North Atlantic Treaty Organization | 1949 | Brussels (BE) | 32 countries | Military alliance: collective defense (Article 5 — "an attack on one is an attack on all"). |
| **WTO** | World Trade Organization | 1995 | Geneva (CH) | 164 countries | Regulates international trade. Sets rules, resolves disputes, reduces tariffs. |
| **UN** | United Nations | 1945 | New York (US) | 193 countries | Maintains peace and security, promotes human rights, coordinates humanitarian aid. |
| **G20** | Group of Twenty | 1999 | No fixed HQ | 19 countries + EU | Forum for economic cooperation between the world's largest economies. |
| **ICC** | International Criminal Court | 2002 | The Hague (NL) | 124 countries | Prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity. |

### Key Differences to Highlight in the Essay

**Political/Military vs. Economic**:
- **NATO** is a military alliance (defense). **WTO** and **G20** are economic. **EU** is both political AND economic.
- The **UN** is the broadest — it aims to maintain peace and human rights globally.

**Binding vs. Advisory**:
- **ICC** prosecutes INDIVIDUALS for specific crimes (genocide, war crimes). Limited jurisdiction.
- **WTO** can authorize trade sanctions. Its rulings are binding.
- **G20** is a discussion forum — its decisions are NOT legally binding.

**Global vs. Regional**:
- **UN, WTO, ICC** → almost universal
- **EU, NATO** → regional (Europe/North America)
- **G20** → selective (largest economies only, but represents ~80% of world GDP)

### Essay Structure (for the exam)

```
Introduction: "International institutions play a crucial role in maintaining global order..."

Body paragraphs (one per institution or grouped):
1. EU — political and economic integration
2. NATO — collective defense
3. WTO — regulating trade
4. UN — peace, security, human rights
5. G20 — economic forum of major economies
6. ICC — justice for international crimes

Conclusion: "While these institutions differ in scope (regional vs global),
purpose (military vs economic vs judicial), and binding power, they all
share the goal of promoting cooperation, stability, and justice worldwide."
```

### Sample Sentences for the Oral Exam

- **EU**: "The European Union is a political and economic union of 27 member states. It allows free movement of people, goods, services, and capital within the single market."
- **NATO**: "NATO is a military alliance founded in 1949. Its core principle is Article 5: an armed attack against one member is considered an attack against all."
- **WTO**: "The World Trade Organization regulates international trade. It provides a framework for negotiating trade agreements and resolving disputes between countries."
- **UN**: "The United Nations was founded in 1945 after World War II. Its main goals are maintaining international peace, protecting human rights, and providing humanitarian aid."
- **G20**: "The G20 is a forum of the world's major economies. It discusses global economic issues like financial stability, climate change, and sustainable development."
- **ICC**: "The International Criminal Court prosecutes individuals for the most serious crimes: genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity. It is a court of last resort."
